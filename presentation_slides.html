Progress Update: Glottal Source Analysis for Pathological Speech

Technical advances completed so far

Author: Irene

Date: February 2026

Focus of this talk: data conditioning, source-filter estimation, engineered descriptors, and classification evidence across IAIF, QCP, and TRLP.

Signal conditioning → Glottal/VT decomposition → Feature engineering → HC vs PD classification

Clinical Speech Challenge and Objective

Challenge

  • Pathological voices show altered phonation, breathiness, and unstable periodicity.
  • Conventional spectral summaries can miss physiological source dynamics.
  • Speaker variability can confound pathology-related signatures.

Objective

  • Recover robust glottal flow-related descriptors from speech.
  • Compare multiple inverse-filtering paradigms under one protocol.
  • Quantify discriminative value for healthy control vs Parkinsonian speech.
Technical emphasis: stable front-end estimation before model complexity.

Signal Conditioning and Framing Strategy

Pre-analysis conditioning

  • Channel consolidation to monophonic waveform.
  • Automatic polarity correction using skewness criterion.
  • Low-frequency drift suppression through high-pass filtering at 50 Hz.
  • Amplitude and temporal consistency checks before decomposition.

Frame design

  • Short-time analysis with 50 ms windows and 10 ms shift.
  • Overlap supports smooth temporal trajectories of glottal estimates.
  • Option to switch toward pitch-adaptive segmentation when needed.
  • Global F0 priors used to stabilize inversion on short frames.
Conditioned waveform→ Framewise inversion→ Glottal-flow proxies→ Statistics per recording

Inverse-Filtering Methods: Technical Summary

IAIF

  • Iterative decomposition alternating source and tract estimates.
  • Low-order glottal modeling + higher-order tract compensation.
  • Works well as a balanced baseline for physiological interpretability.

QCP

  • Closed-phase focused tract estimation to reduce source leakage.
  • More selective use of glottal cycle regions.
  • Produces stable tract/source separation in strongly voiced segments.

TRLP

  • Recursive linear prediction with temporal coefficient continuity.
  • Residual-driven glottal flow trend reconstruction via leaky integration.
  • Useful for continuity constraints, but sensitive to noise/model mismatch.

Engineered Feature Space

Glottal quotients

NAQ QOQ

Capture opening/closing behavior and pulse-shape asymmetry.

Spectral balance

HRF H1H2

Reflect breathiness, tension, and glottal spectral tilt patterns.

Energy + dynamics

G_RMS G_ZCR G_CREST DG_PEAK RES_RMS

Quantify excitation intensity, irregularity, and residual structure.

Complementary acoustic baseline: MFCC-derived descriptors for broader spectral envelope information.

Evaluation Design for Reliable Clinical Signal

Validation protocol

  • Nested cross-validation with speaker-group separation.
  • Outer folds estimate generalization; inner folds tune hyperparameters.
  • Prevents speaker leakage between train and test partitions.

Reported views

  • Sample-level performance (frame/segment perspective).
  • Speaker-level performance (clinical decision perspective).
  • Probability aggregation by speaker for stable subject estimates.

Interpretation

  • Strong jump from glottal-only to glottal+MFCC in all three methods.
  • QCP+SVM reaches the highest speaker AUC (0.912) among completed runs.
  • RF provides stable high speaker metrics across methods once MFCC is added.
  • Direct-only additions did not change performance relative to glottal-only in these runs.
  • Conclusion: complementary acoustic context remains essential for strongest discrimination.

Comparative Findings Across IAIF, QCP, TRLP

Glottal-only regime

  • Moderate discrimination (speaker AUC roughly 0.64 to 0.71).
  • QCP generally strongest in this constrained setting.
  • TRLP shows weaker sample-level behavior without acoustic complement.

Glottal + acoustic regime

  • Speaker AUC rises to approximately 0.87 to 0.91.
  • Speaker accuracy consolidates around 0.78 to 0.80.
  • Variance across folds decreases relative to glottal-only setup.

Technical implication

Glottal descriptors carry clinically relevant signal, but their strongest impact appears when fused with broad acoustic descriptors that preserve contextual phonetic structure.

Methodological Notes and Ongoing Work

Current confidence in results

  • Speaker-grouped validation supports realistic subject-level generalization.
  • Consistent ranking patterns across IAIF/QCP/TRLP increase robustness of conclusions.
  • Both sample-level and speaker-level views tell a coherent story.

Open analysis lines

Potential Integration (One Slide): Early-Exit ASR

Why this could help

  • Better front-end representations can reduce uncertainty at shallow encoder depths.
  • Difficult pathological segments can still be routed to deeper layers.
  • Expected effect: improved efficiency-robustness tradeoff on edge hardware.

Minimal integration concept

  • Front-end fusion of acoustic and glottal descriptors.
  • Confidence head observes both linguistic posterior and signal-quality cues.
  • Analyze exit-depth distribution across healthy vs pathological speech.

Planned validation target

  • Keep current clinical classification findings as the evidence base.
  • Use them to motivate a later ASR transfer study, not to replace the current work focus.

Conclusions

What is established

  • A complete and technically grounded glottal analysis workflow is in place.
  • QCP and IAIF show strong discriminative behavior when combined with acoustic descriptors.
  • Speaker-level metrics reach high values for vowel-task pathology discrimination.
  • Results are consistent across two strong baseline classifiers.

Immediate next update

  • Add ongoing XGBoost outcomes for direct comparison with SVM and RF.
  • Expand error analysis by subgroup and phonation characteristics.
  • Refine feature-importance interpretation at speaker level.
